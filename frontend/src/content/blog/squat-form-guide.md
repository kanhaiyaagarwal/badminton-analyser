# How to Do Squats With Proper Form (Complete Beginner Guide)

![Proper squat form](/blog/squat-form-hero.jpg)

The squat is the single most effective lower body exercise you can do. It works your quads, glutes, hamstrings, and core all at once — and you don't need any equipment to start.

But most people squat wrong. Knees caving in, heels lifting off the floor, back rounding at the bottom. These mistakes don't just kill your results — they set you up for knee and back injuries that can take months to recover from.

This guide covers everything you need to know about proper squat form, the most common mistakes, and how AI tracking can help you fix problems you can't see on your own.

## Why Squat Form Is Non-Negotiable

Unlike pushups, where bad form mostly wastes your effort, **bad squat form actively hurts you.** Your knees and lower back are under heavy load at the bottom of every rep. If your mechanics are off, that load goes to your joints instead of your muscles.

Here's the thing most people miss: squats aren't naturally dangerous. Your body was designed to squat. Watch any toddler pick something up off the floor — perfect squat form, every time. The problem is that years of sitting in chairs has made most adults forget how to do it properly.

Good form isn't about memorizing a checklist. It's about relearning a movement pattern your body already knows.

## The Perfect Bodyweight Squat: Step by Step

![Step by step squat form](/blog/squat-proper-form.jpg)

### 1. Set Your Stance

Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart or slightly wider. Point your toes out about 15-30 degrees — not straight forward. Your feet should feel planted and stable.

**Why toes out?** Your hip anatomy requires it. Squatting with toes straight forward forces your knees into an unnatural path. The slight toe-out lets your knees track over your toes naturally.

### 2. Brace Your Core

Before you descend, take a deep breath into your belly (not your chest) and tighten your abs. Think about bracing for a punch. This creates intra-abdominal pressure that stabilizes your spine throughout the movement.

This single step prevents more back injuries than any other cue. Don't skip it.

### 3. Initiate With Your Hips

Start the squat by pushing your hips back — like you're sitting into a chair that's just slightly too far behind you. Then bend your knees to lower yourself down.

**Common mistake:** Starting by bending your knees forward. This shifts your weight to your toes and puts excessive stress on your knee joints. Hips back first, always.

### 4. The Descent

Lower yourself until your hip crease drops below your knee — this is "parallel" depth. Your thighs should be at least parallel to the floor.

As you descend:
- **Knees** track over your toes (same direction your toes point)
- **Chest** stays up — imagine a string pulling your sternum to the ceiling
- **Heels** stay planted firmly on the ground
- **Back** maintains its natural curve — no rounding

If your heels lift off the floor, your ankle mobility needs work. Elevate your heels on small plates or a folded towel until your flexibility improves.

### 5. The Bottom Position

At the bottom of the squat, check these three things:
1. **Weight is in your mid-foot and heels** — not your toes
2. **Knees are pushed out** — not collapsing inward
3. **Chest is up** — your torso should be as upright as your mobility allows

Don't bounce at the bottom. A controlled pause (even half a second) ensures you're using muscle, not momentum.

### 6. Standing Back Up

Drive through your heels to stand up. Push the floor away from you. Your hips and shoulders should rise at the same rate — if your hips shoot up first and your chest drops forward, the weight is too far forward.

Squeeze your glutes at the top to fully extend your hips. This completes the rep and reinforces proper hip mechanics.

### 7. Breathing

- **Inhale** and brace before you descend
- **Hold** your breath through the bottom (this maintains core pressure)
- **Exhale** as you stand up past the sticking point

For bodyweight squats, you can exhale on the way up from the start. The breath hold matters more when you add weight.

## The 5 Most Common Squat Mistakes

![Common squat mistakes](/blog/squat-mistakes.jpg)

### 1. Knee Cave (Valgus Collapse)

**What it looks like:** Your knees collapse inward as you descend or push out of the bottom.

**Why it's bad:** Puts extreme stress on your ACL and MCL. This is the number one cause of squat-related knee injuries.

**The fix:** Actively push your knees out over your toes throughout the entire movement. Think about "spreading the floor apart" with your feet. If your knees still cave, your glutes are weak — add glute bridges and banded squats to your warm-up.

### 2. Butt Wink

**What it looks like:** Your pelvis tucks under at the bottom of the squat, rounding your lower back.

**Why it's bad:** Puts your lumbar spine in flexion under load. Over time, this can cause disc issues.

**The fix:** Only squat as deep as you can while maintaining a neutral spine. For most people, this means improving hip and ankle mobility. Elevating your heels often helps immediately. Don't force depth at the expense of your back position.

### 3. Heels Rising

**What it looks like:** Your heels lift off the ground as you squat down.

**Why it's bad:** Shifts all the load to your knees and toes. You lose stability and power.

**The fix:** This is almost always an ankle mobility issue. Stretch your calves daily, and use a heel elevation (1-2 inches) while you work on flexibility. Also try widening your stance slightly.

### 4. Forward Lean (Good Morning Squat)

**What it looks like:** Your chest drops forward and your hips shoot back, turning the squat into a hip hinge.

**Why it's bad:** Shifts the work from your quads to your lower back. Your back isn't built to be the primary mover in a squat.

**The fix:** Keep your chest proud. Look at a spot on the wall at eye level. Arms can extend forward for counterbalance. If you still lean excessively, your quads may be weak relative to your posterior chain — front squats and goblet squats will fix this.

### 5. Half Squats

**What it looks like:** You bend your knees slightly and stand back up without ever reaching parallel.

**Why it's bad:** You're only training a fraction of the range of motion. Partial squats don't build the glute and hamstring strength that comes from full depth. And counterintuitively, partial squats are *harder* on your knees because you reverse direction at the point of maximum knee stress.

**The fix:** Full depth, every rep. If you can't hit parallel with good form, regress to box squats or goblet squats with a counterbalance. Build mobility and strength through the full range before adding speed or volume.

## Squat Progressions (Beginner to Advanced)

![Squat progressions](/blog/squat-progression.jpg)

### Level 1: Chair Squats

Squat down to a chair or bench and stand back up. The chair gives you a depth target and the confidence that you won't fall. Focus on perfect form — hips back, knees out, chest up.

### Level 2: Goblet Squats

Hold a water bottle, book, or light weight at your chest. The counterbalance actually makes it *easier* to maintain an upright torso. This is the best squat variation for learning proper mechanics.

### Level 3: Bodyweight Squats

The standard air squat. Master this with full depth and perfect form before moving on. Aim for sets of 20 with consistent technique.

### Level 4: Jump Squats

Add an explosive jump at the top. Land softly, absorb with your legs, and immediately descend into the next rep. Builds power and cardio simultaneously.

### Level 5: Pistol Squat Progressions

Single-leg squats are the ultimate bodyweight leg exercise. Start with assisted versions (holding a doorframe or TRX strap) and work toward unassisted pistols over months.

## Your First Form-Checked Squat Workout

![Home squat workout](/blog/home-workout.jpg)

Ready to test your form? Here's a simple workout with AI tracking:

**Warm-up:**
- 20 bodyweight hip circles (10 each direction)
- 10 leg swings per side (forward and lateral)
- 10 slow goblet squats with a pause at the bottom

**Workout:**
- **Set 1:** 15 squats at a 3-1-2 tempo (3 seconds down, 1 pause, 2 up)
- Rest 60 seconds
- **Set 2:** 15 squats at normal tempo, focusing on depth
- Rest 60 seconds
- **Set 3:** Max reps with perfect form (stop when form breaks)

**Track these numbers:**
- Total reps across all sets
- Form score per set
- Depth consistency — are you hitting parallel every rep?
- Where your form broke down first (knees, depth, or back angle)

Do this twice a week. When you can hit 3 sets of 20 with a high form score and consistent depth, you're ready for jump squats or single-leg progressions.

The goal isn't just more reps — it's reps you can be confident are building muscle and protecting your joints. AI form tracking gives you that confidence.

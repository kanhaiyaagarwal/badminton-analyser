# How to Do Pushups With Proper Form (AI Form Check Guide)

![Proper pushup form](/blog-images/pushup-form-hero.jpg)

Most people think they know how to do a pushup. Most people are wrong.

Bad pushup form doesn't just limit your gains. It shifts stress from your chest and triceps to your shoulders, wrists, and lower back. Do enough bad reps and you'll feel it — not in the muscles you're trying to build, but in the joints you're trying to protect.

This guide covers proper pushup form step by step, the 5 mistakes that cause the most injuries, and how AI camera tracking catches problems your mirror can't.

## Why Form Matters More Than Reps

Here's the truth most fitness content won't tell you: **10 pushups with perfect form will build more muscle than 30 sloppy ones.**

When your form breaks down, the load shifts from your chest and triceps to your joints. Your shoulders take the hit. Your lower back compensates. You "complete" the rep, but the muscles you're trying to target barely worked.

Worse, you're reinforcing bad movement patterns. Every bad rep makes the next one more likely to be bad too.

## The Perfect Pushup: Step by Step

![Step by step pushup form](/blog-images/pushup-proper-form.jpg)

### 1. Set Your Hands

Place your hands slightly wider than shoulder-width apart, fingers spread and pointing forward. Your weight should be distributed across your entire palm — not dumped into the heel of your hand.

**Common mistake:** Hands too wide. This turns it into a shoulder exercise and limits your range of motion. If your hands are wider than 1.5x your shoulder width, bring them in.

### 2. Lock Your Body Position

Before you even start the rep, get your body right:

- **Feet** together or hip-width apart (closer = harder)
- **Core** braced — squeeze your abs like someone's about to poke your stomach
- **Glutes** tight — this prevents your hips from sagging
- **Head** neutral — look at a spot about 6 inches in front of your hands

Your body should form a perfectly straight line from the crown of your head to your heels. No sagging. No piking. If someone laid a broomstick on your back, it should touch your head, upper back, and glutes simultaneously.

### 3. The Descent

Lower yourself by bending your elbows. Here's the critical part most people get wrong:

**Your elbows should angle back at about 45 degrees from your torso** — not flared straight out to the sides at 90 degrees.

Think of it as making an arrow shape (↑) with your body, not a T shape. Flaring to 90 degrees puts enormous stress on your shoulder joint and rotator cuff.

Go down until your chest is about a fist-height from the floor. Your upper arms should be at least parallel to the ground. If you're not going this low, you're doing half reps.

### 4. The Push

Drive through your entire palm to push back up. Think about pushing the floor away from you rather than pushing yourself up — this mental cue helps engage your chest more.

Fully extend your arms at the top. Don't lock your elbows aggressively, but do straighten them. A full rep means full range of motion — top to bottom, bottom to top.

### 5. Breathing

- **Inhale** on the way down
- **Exhale** on the way up (the hard part)

Don't hold your breath. This spikes your blood pressure and reduces your endurance.

## The 5 Most Common Pushup Mistakes

![Common pushup mistakes to avoid](/blog-images/pushup-mistakes.jpg)

### 1. Sagging Hips (The "Banana Back")

**What it looks like:** Your hips drop toward the floor, creating a curve in your lower back.

**Why it's bad:** Compresses your lumbar spine. You'll feel it in your lower back instead of your chest.

**The fix:** Squeeze your glutes and brace your core before every set. If you can't maintain a straight body, drop to your knees — a proper knee pushup beats a sloppy full pushup every time.

### 2. Flared Elbows

**What it looks like:** Your elbows point straight out to the sides, making a T shape with your body.

**Why it's bad:** Puts your shoulder in an impinged position. Over time, this leads to rotator cuff issues.

**The fix:** Tuck your elbows to 45 degrees. It might feel weaker at first because your chest has to work harder — that's the point.

### 3. Half Reps

**What it looks like:** You bend your elbows slightly and push back up without ever getting your chest near the floor.

**Why it's bad:** You're only working through a fraction of the range of motion, which means a fraction of the muscle activation.

**The fix:** Touch your chest to the floor or a fist-height above it on every rep. If you can't do full-depth reps, reduce the difficulty (incline pushups, knee pushups) rather than cheating the range.

### 4. Head Drop

**What it looks like:** Your head hangs down, chin to chest, breaking the line of your spine.

**Why it's bad:** Puts strain on your neck and disrupts your body alignment.

**The fix:** Keep your neck neutral. Pick a spot on the floor about 6 inches ahead of your fingertips and keep your eyes there throughout the set.

### 5. Speed Repping

**What it looks like:** Bouncing up and down as fast as possible, using momentum instead of muscle.

**Why it's bad:** Momentum does the work, not your muscles. Plus, you lose control at the bottom, which is where shoulder injuries happen.

**The fix:** Use a 2-1-2 tempo. 2 seconds down, 1 second pause at the bottom, 2 seconds up. You'll do fewer reps, but each one will actually count.

## Pushup Progressions (Start Where You Are)

![Pushup progressions from beginner to advanced](/blog-images/pushup-progression.jpg)

Not ready for full pushups? Here's the progression from easiest to hardest:

### Level 1: Wall Pushups
Stand arm's length from a wall. Perform pushups against the wall. Focus on perfect form — straight body, proper elbow angle, full range of motion.

### Level 2: Incline Pushups
Use a bench, table, or sturdy chair. The higher the surface, the easier the pushup. Gradually lower the surface as you get stronger.

### Level 3: Knee Pushups
On the floor, but with your knees down. Keep your body straight from knees to head. Don't let your hips sag just because your knees are down.

### Level 4: Full Pushups
The standard pushup as described in this guide. Master this before moving to advanced variations.

### Level 5: Advanced Variations
- **Diamond pushups** — hands close together, targets triceps
- **Archer pushups** — one arm extended wide, targets one side at a time
- **Decline pushups** — feet elevated, targets upper chest
- **Plyometric pushups** — explosive push with hands leaving the ground

## Your First Form-Checked Workout

![Home workout with phone](/blog-images/home-workout.jpg)

Ready to see how your form actually looks? Here's a simple workout to try with AI form tracking:

**Warm-up:** 10 arm circles forward, 10 arm circles backward, 10 shoulder taps in plank position.

**Workout:**
- **Set 1:** Max reps with perfect form (stop when form breaks down)
- Rest 60 seconds
- **Set 2:** Max reps with perfect form
- Rest 60 seconds
- **Set 3:** Max reps with perfect form

**Track these numbers:**
- Total reps across all 3 sets
- Form score per set
- Which set your form started breaking down

Do this twice a week. When you can hit 3 sets of 15+ with a high form score, you're ready to progress to a harder variation.

The key isn't how many pushups you can do — it's how many you can do *well*. Start there, and the numbers will follow.
